## 2. The Compound Interest Formula {#formula}

The fundamental compound interest formula is:

**A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)**

Where:
- **A** = Final amount (future value)
- **P** = Principal (starting amount)
- **r** = Annual interest rate (as a decimal — 7% = 0.07)
- **n** = Number of times interest compounds per year
- **t** = Time in years

**Example:** $5,000 (P) invested at 6% annual return (r = 0.06), compounding monthly (n = 12), for 20 years (t = 20):

A = 5,000 × (1 + 0.06/12)^(12 × 20)
A = 5,000 × (1.005)^240
A = 5,000 × 3.3102
**A = $16,551**

The $5,000 principal becomes $16,551 — a gain of $11,551 earned purely through compounding, with no additional contributions.

### For Regular Contributions (Annuity Formula)

When you add regular contributions, the formula extends:

**FV = PMT × [(1 + r/n)^(nt) − 1] / (r/n)**

Where PMT is the regular payment per period.

**Combined formula** (starting principal + regular contributions):

**Total FV = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) + PMT × [(1 + r/n)^(nt) − 1] / (r/n)**

The BankDeMark calculator applies this automatically — you never need to calculate it manually.

## 3. Compound Interest Calculator Inputs Explained {#inputs}

### Starting Principal

The amount you are investing today. This can be:
- A savings account balance you are putting to work
- An initial investment in a TFSA, RRSP, or brokerage account
- An existing portfolio balance
- Zero (if you are starting from scratch with just regular contributions)

**Canada/U.S. note:** Select CAD for Canadian accounts, USD for U.S. accounts. If you hold both, run separate calculations.

### Regular Contribution

The amount you plan to add periodically — typically monthly. Regular contributions are the second engine of compound growth (compounding is the first). Most long-term wealth is built through the combination of an initial investment and consistent ongoing contributions.

**Leave this at zero** if you want to see pure compounding on a lump sum without additions.

## 4. Reading Your Results: What the Numbers Mean {#results}

The compound interest calculator returns several key outputs:

### Final Balance

The total value of your investment at the end of your specified time horizon. This is the headline number — what your investment becomes through compounding.

### Total Principal Contributed

The sum of your starting investment plus all regular contributions over the time period. This is the total of your own money invested.

### Total Interest Earned

Final Balance minus Total Principal. This is the compounding effect — money your money earned without any additional action from you. In long time horizons and at reasonable return rates, this number consistently exceeds the total contributions — often dramatically.

**The crossover point:** In long-term investment projections, there is typically a crossover point where cumulative interest earned overtakes total contributions. For a $500/month investment at 7% over 30 years, the crossover occurs around year 20. After that point, compounding is producing more wealth than new contributions.

### Year-by-Year Breakdown

The growth table showing your balance at the end of each year. This table is important because it makes the exponential curve visible. The first year adds a modest amount. By year 20, annual growth may exceed what you contributed in the entire first decade.

### Interest-to-Principal Ratio

How many times your original investment has been multiplied by compounding. A ratio of 4x means that for every dollar you invested, compounding produced three additional dollars.

## 5. Compounding Frequency: Daily vs. Monthly vs. Annual {#frequency}

The same principal and rate produce different results depending on how often interest compounds. More frequent compounding means interest is calculated on a larger balance more often — producing slightly more growth.

### Frequency Comparison: $10,000 at 6% over 10 Years

| Compounding Frequency | Final Balance | Difference from Annual |
|----------------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Daily (365/year) | $18,220 | +$56 |
| Monthly (12/year) | $18,194 | +$30 |
| Quarterly (4/year) | $18,061 | +$97 less |
| Annually (1/year) | $17,908 | baseline |

For long-term investment projections, the choice between daily and monthly compounding has minimal practical impact. The difference becomes more significant over very long horizons or with very large principals.

**Practical implication:** For savings accounts and GICs, use the compounding frequency stated in the product terms. For investment accounts (stocks, ETFs, index funds), compounding is effectively continuous — use daily or monthly for the most accurate projection.

## 6. The Impact of Regular Contributions {#contributions}

Regular contributions — monthly savings, TFSA contributions, RRSP contributions, investment account deposits — are the second engine of compound growth alongside the starting principal.

The interaction between a starting balance and regular contributions creates particularly powerful compounding. Each new contribution begins compounding from the moment it is added, while the existing balance continues compounding simultaneously.

### Contribution Amount Comparison: 30 Years at 7%

| Monthly Contribution | Starting Principal | Final Balance | Total Contributions | Interest Earned |
|--------------------|--------------------|--------------|--------------------|----|
| $0 | $50,000 | $380,613 | $50,000 | $330,613 |
| $250 | $50,000 | $665,867 | $140,000 | $525,867 |
| $500 | $50,000 | $951,121 | $230,000 | $721,121 |
| $1,000 | $50,000 | $1,521,628 | $410,000 | $1,111,628 |
| $500 | $0 | $570,507 | $180,000 | $390,507 |

*Approximate figures. Monthly compounding at 7% annual return.*

Key observations:
1. Even a $50,000 starting principal with no contributions grows to $380,000 in 30 years purely through compounding
2. Adding $500/month to $50,000 produces a $1,521,628 result vs. $570,507 starting from zero — the starting principal's compounding advantage is substantial
3. The interest earned ($1,111,628 at $1,000/month + $50,000 starting) far exceeds total contributions ($410,000) — this is the essence of compound growth

## 8. Compound Interest on Debt: The Reverse Effect {#debt}

Compound interest works both for you (on investments) and against you (on debt). On savings, it builds wealth. On debt, it extracts it.

### How Compound Interest Destroys Wealth on High-Rate Debt

**Credit card example:** $5,000 balance at 19.99% APR, minimum payments only (approximately 2% of balance or $10, whichever is greater).

At minimum payments, this debt takes approximately 20 years to pay off and costs over $8,000 in interest — significantly more than the original $5,000 balance [SOURCE NEEDED]. The compound interest calculator, used in reverse, makes this devastatingly clear.

### The Investment vs. Debt Rate Comparison

Before investing, compare your expected investment return to your debt interest rate:

| Debt Type | Typical Rate | Action |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Credit cards | 19–22% | Pay off before any non-emergency investing |
| Personal loans | 7–15% | Generally prioritize payoff |
| Car loans | 4–8% | Case-by-case vs. investing |
| Student loans | 4–8% (varies) | Case-by-case vs. investing |
| Mortgage (current rates) | 4–6% | Invest simultaneously (low guaranteed return from paydown) |
| HELOC | 6–8% | Case-by-case |

The logic: no investment reliably returns 19.99% per year. Paying off credit card debt is the only guaranteed 19.99% return available to most people.

## 9. The Rule of 72: A Compound Interest Shortcut {#rule-of-72}

The Rule of 72 is a useful mental shortcut for estimating how long it takes to double money at compound interest:

**Years to double = 72 ÷ Annual Return Rate**

| Annual Return | Years to Double |
|--------------|----------------|
| 2% | 36 years |
| 3% | 24 years |
| 4% | 18 years |
| 5% | 14.4 years |
| 6% | 12 years |
| 7% | ~10.3 years |
| 8% | 9 years |
| 10% | 7.2 years |
| 12% | 6 years |

**How to use the Rule of 72:**

*"I have $100,000 in my RRSP at age 40. If I earn 7%, when will it double without any additional contributions?"*

72 ÷ 7 = ~10.3 years. It doubles to ~$200,000 by age 50. Doubles again to ~$400,000 by age 60. By 65 (25 years), approximately $560,000 — from $100,000 with no contributions.

The Rule of 72 also applies to inflation. At 3% inflation, your purchasing power halves every 24 years. This is why investing is not optional for long-term financial security — money sitting in cash loses half its purchasing power in 24 years.

## 10. Compound Interest in Canadian Registered Accounts {#canada}

The most powerful financial tool available to Canadian investors is the combination of compound interest and tax-free or tax-deferred growth inside registered accounts.

### TFSA: Tax-Free Compounding

Inside a TFSA, compound interest works at full speed — no annual tax drag on dividends, interest, or capital gains. Outside a TFSA, in a non-registered account, the tax drag on investment returns reduces effective compound growth each year.

**Tax drag illustration over 20 years:**

Assume a $50,000 investment at 7% nominal return (dividends and capital gains, simplified marginal tax rate of 30%):

- **TFSA (tax-free):** After 20 years: ~$193,000
- **Non-registered (30% annual tax drag):** After 20 years: approximately $155,000–$175,000 depending on income type and realization timing

The TFSA advantage over 20 years is $20,000–$40,000 on a $50,000 starting investment — purely from compounding without the tax drag [SOURCE NEEDED — tax calculation is simplified illustration].

**TFSA cumulative contribution room as of 2026:** $95,000 for those eligible since 2009 [SOURCE NEEDED].

**Strategy implication:** Place the highest-expected-return assets inside your TFSA first. The tax-free compounding advantage is greatest on assets with the highest growth rates.

[→ Maximize your TFSA: Registered Account Calculator](/calculators/registered-account-calculator)
[→ Canadian compound interest deep dive: Compound Interest Canada](/compound-interest-canada)

### RRSP: Tax-Deferred Compounding

Inside an RRSP, compound interest also works without annual tax drag. The tax is deferred to withdrawal. The advantage of deferral is that the full pre-tax return compounds throughout the accumulation phase — you are investing the government's portion of your money alongside your own.

**RRSP compounding advantage:**

If your marginal tax rate is 40%, a $1,000 contribution to an RRSP generates a $400 tax refund. This $400 is essentially an immediate 40% return on the contribution, before any investment growth.

Invested for 30 years at 7%, that $400 refund (invested) becomes approximately $3,050. The full $1,000 contribution (the $600 after-tax you provided + the $400 government portion deferred) compounds to approximately $7,612.

[→ Canada-specific guide: Compound Interest in Canada](/compound-interest-canada)

### GICs and HISAs: Compound Interest on Guaranteed Products

For Canadians who prefer guaranteed instruments:

**GIC (Guaranteed Investment Certificate):** Fixed-rate, fixed-term products issued by banks and credit unions. Interest is typically compounded annually. Rates in 2026 range from approximately 3–5% depending on term and institution [SOURCE NEEDED].

**HISA (High-Interest Savings Account):** Online banks (EQ Bank, Oaken Financial, Simplii) typically offer higher rates than Big Five banks for HISA products [SOURCE NEEDED]. Interest is typically compounded daily or monthly and paid monthly.

The compound interest calculator works for both — input the stated rate and the compounding frequency from the product terms.

## 11. Compound Interest for U.S. Investors {#usa}

### 401(k) and IRA: Tax-Advantaged Compounding

U.S. investors benefit from compound interest tax advantages through 401(k) and IRA accounts:

**Traditional 401(k)/IRA:** Pre-tax contributions. Compounding occurs without annual tax drag. Withdrawals taxed as ordinary income in retirement.

**Roth 401(k)/IRA:** Post-tax contributions. Compounding occurs tax-free. Qualified withdrawals tax-free — structurally equivalent to the Canadian TFSA.

**2024 contribution limits:**
- 401(k): $23,000 ($30,500 with catch-up for 50+) [SOURCE NEEDED]
- IRA/Roth IRA: $7,000 ($8,000 with catch-up for 50+) [SOURCE NEEDED]

## 12. How to Maximize Compound Interest in Your Portfolio {#maximize}

### 1. Start Immediately

Every year of delay reduces the compounding base. A 10-year delay costs more in final balance than 10 years of contributions can recover, in most scenarios. The compound interest calculator is the most compelling argument for immediate action.

### 2. Maximize Tax-Sheltered Accounts First

Compound interest works at full speed inside TFSA, RRSP, and FHSA (Canada) or Roth IRA, 401(k), and HSA (U.S.). Outside these accounts, annual tax drag on dividends and interest reduces effective compound rate. Always maximize registered account contributions before investing non-registered.

**Priority order for most Canadians:**
1. TFSA (fully tax-free, flexible, no tax on withdrawal)
2. RRSP (if marginal tax rate is 30%+, the deduction provides immediate return)
3. FHSA (if first-time homebuyer, combination of both TFSA and RRSP tax benefits)
4. Non-registered investment account

### 3. Minimize Investment Fees

Investment fees compound against you exactly as returns compound for you. A 1.5% MER vs. 0.2% MER (a typical difference between actively managed mutual funds and index ETFs) reduces the effective compound rate by 1.3 percentage points.

Over 30 years on $200,000:
- At 7% (gross) − 0.2% fee = 6.8% net: ~$1,339,000
- At 7% (gross) − 1.5% fee = 5.5% net: ~$974,000

The fee difference costs approximately $365,000 in final balance on a $200,000 starting investment [SOURCE NEEDED — illustrative calculation].

### 4. Automate Contributions

Behavioral consistency matters more than optimization. An automated $500/month contribution at a 6% return produces more wealth than an irregular $700/month contribution at 7% that often lapses. Automating removes the decision from the equation.

### 5. Reinvest Dividends

Dividend-paying equities and funds distribute cash. Reinvesting dividends — automatically buying additional shares — adds to the compounding base. Dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs) are available through most Canadian and U.S. brokerages.

### 6. Do Not Interrupt Compounding

Withdrawing from investments to cover expenses interrupts the compounding curve at exactly the moment when it has the most momentum. This is the core reason for maintaining an emergency fund — so that unexpected expenses never require investment liquidation.

## 13. Compound Interest Myths and Misconceptions {#myths}

### Myth 1: "You need a lot of money to start"

False. The compound interest calculator consistently shows that the amount of money you start with matters less than time and consistency. $100/month for 40 years at 7% produces more than $10,000 as a lump sum for 20 years. Starting with whatever you have — even small amounts — is always superior to waiting until you have "enough" to start.

[→ See the math: How Much Will $100 a Month Grow?](/how-much-will-100-a-month-grow)

### Myth 2: "Compound interest is too slow to matter for debt"

False. Credit card compound interest at 19.99% is extremely fast. Minimum payments on a $10,000 credit card balance at 19.99% take roughly 14–17 years and cost nearly $15,000 in interest [SOURCE NEEDED]. Compound interest on debt is as powerful as compound interest on investments — but it works against you.

### Myth 3: "A higher return is the most important variable"

Not for most people. Return rate matters, but contribution consistency and time horizon matter more for most wealth-building scenarios. The difference between a 6% and 8% return over 30 years on $500/month is significant — but not as significant as 10 additional years of contributions at 6%.

The compound interest calculator makes this sensitivity analysis immediate: change the return assumption and see the impact; then change the time horizon by 5 years and see that the impact is often larger.

### Myth 4: "I can catch up later"

The mathematics of compound interest make catching up extremely expensive. The $800,000 difference in final balance between starting at 25 vs. 35 (from the Sarah/Michael example earlier) cannot be closed by higher contributions alone without substantially increasing the monthly contribution amount for the entire remaining horizon.

### Myth 5: "Simple interest is basically the same as compound interest"

For very short terms, the difference is small. For long terms, the difference is enormous. $10,000 at 7% simple interest for 30 years = $31,000. $10,000 at 7% compound interest for 30 years = $76,000. Compound interest produces 2.5 times more wealth over 30 years at the same nominal rate.

## 15. FAQ: Compound Interest Calculator {#faq}

### What is compound interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both your original principal and all previously accumulated interest. Unlike simple interest (which calculates only on the original principal), compound interest causes balances to grow exponentially over time because each interest payment becomes part of the principal for the next calculation period.

[→ Full definition and explanation: What Is Compound Interest?](/what-is-compound-interest)

### What is the compound interest formula?

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where A is the final amount, P is the principal, r is the annual rate, n is the compounding frequency per year, and t is time in years. For regular contributions, the annuity formula FV = PMT × [(1+r/n)^(nt) − 1] / (r/n) is added.

[→ Full formula derivation and worked examples: Compound Interest Formula](/compound-interest-formula)

### What return rate should I use in the compound interest calculator?

Use a return rate that reflects your actual investment portfolio. For a diversified equity ETF portfolio: 6–7% is a conservative long-term assumption. For a balanced 60/40 portfolio: 5–6%. For a savings account or GIC: use the stated rate. Avoid using historical maximum returns (10–11% S&P 500) for long-term retirement projections — conservative assumptions produce better-grounded decisions.

### Does compound interest really double money every decade?

At approximately 7% annual return, the Rule of 72 suggests money doubles every ~10.3 years. At 8%, it doubles every 9 years. This is a mathematical fact, not a guarantee — it assumes a consistent return rate, which investments do not produce in practice. Long-term averages are the basis for projections, and actual results will vary year to year.

### What is the difference between daily and monthly compounding?

Daily compounding calculates and adds interest every day; monthly does so once per month. Daily compounding produces slightly more growth because interest is being added to the balance more frequently, creating a larger base for subsequent calculations. In practice, for long-term investment projections, the difference is small — typically less than 0.1% per year.

[→ Detailed comparison: Daily vs. Monthly Compound Interest](/daily-compound-interest)

### How long does it take to reach $1 million through compound interest?

It depends on your starting balance, monthly contribution, and return rate. At $1,000/month with a $0 starting balance at 7%: approximately 30 years. With a $50,000 starting balance and $1,000/month at 7%: approximately 25 years. With a $100,000 starting balance and $2,000/month at 7%: approximately 18 years.

[→ Full timeline analysis: How Long to Reach $1 Million Investing](/how-long-to-reach-1-million-investing)

### How much will $500 a month grow with compound interest?

At 7% annual return, compounded monthly, $500/month grows to approximately $262,000 after 20 years and $611,000 after 30 years. Total contributions: $120,000 (20 years) or $180,000 (30 years). The remaining $142,000 (20-year) or $431,000 (30-year) is pure compounding.

### Does compound interest apply to Canadian GICs?

Yes. GICs pay interest that compounds according to the terms stated in the product — typically annually or at maturity. The compound interest calculator works for GIC projections — input the stated interest rate and the appropriate compounding frequency (usually annual for traditional GICs).
